The big picture

The machine that moves the world

Supply chain and logistics is the choreography of moving physical things from where they're made to where they're wanted — materials into factories, goods into warehouses, parcels onto doorsteps, and returns back again. Logistics is the moving and storing; the supply chain is the whole web of suppliers, plants, carriers and customers behind it. It's the system nobody notices until it breaks — a ship wedged in the Suez Canal, a port jammed for weeks — and then the whole world feels it at once.

The scale is hard to overstate. Moving and storing goods cost U.S. businesses about $2.58 trillion in 2024 — roughly 8.8% of the economy — and that's one country. Around 80% of world trade by volume still goes by sea, threaded through a handful of narrow chokepoints. It's cyclical and exposed, too: a fuel spike, a driver shortage or a closed strait can ripple through every shelf within weeks.

For most of its history this was a world of ships, trucks and clipboards. Now it's one of software's most interesting frontiers — a system for every step, planning to delivery, already worth tens of billions a year. The newest layer is AI that doesn't just report but decides and acts: Gartner sees agentic supply-chain software jumping from under $2 billion in 2025 to about $53 billion by 2030.

That's the territory this digest maps. It follows one shipment through twelve milestones — forecast, sourcing, making, storing, moving, crossing borders, the doorstep, and the trip back as a return — across the five ways goods travel: ocean, road, rail, air and parcel. Beneath each sit the math that makes it work and the companies building it, tied together by three ideas: the triangle of service, cost and cash; the stack from system-of-record to orchestration; and the shift from recording, to executing, to seeing, to deciding.

How to think about this market

The easiest way to read this market is as a pyramid. At the base sits the system of record — the ERP (SAP, Oracle) that holds the orders, inventory and money. On top of it sit execution systems, each running one job: a TMS for transport, a WMS for the warehouse, a YMS for the yard, an OMS for orders, and source-to-pay for buying. Above those are the planning and visibility layers that look across all the functions at once. And right at the top is the new orchestration layer — control towers and AI agents that coordinate decisions across everything below. For decades each layer was a separate product from a separate vendor; the big fight of the next ten years is where the value, and the budget, end up — in broad all-in-one suites, in the orchestration layer on top, or with best-of-breed specialists.

A quick look through Porter's five forces explains why the economics work the way they do. Buyers have real power — big shippers can run bake-offs and threaten to build it themselves — but once a warehouse or planning system is embedded, switching is so painful that incumbents keep the revenue anyway. New entrants struggle in data-network businesses like visibility, where years of carrier connections build a moat nobody can copy quickly, yet they thrive in narrow point tools, which keeps a long tail of startups alive. The main substitute is still "the spreadsheet and the phone" — the real enemy is inertia, not a rival vendor. Suppliers (cloud, and increasingly the AI-model providers) are gaining power as AI moves to the centre of the product. And rivalry is heating up as suites, ERPs and specialists all chase the same agentic-AI story.

Two forces pull in opposite directions. Consolidation shows up in the deal-making — WiseTech buying e2open, DSV buying DB Schenker, Blue Yonder buying One Network and Pledge, the big ERPs reaching into every nearby module — because scale, data and having a single vendor to hold accountable all reward breadth. But fragmentation sticks around because supply chains are so different from one another: a pharma cold-chain, a fashion retailer and a bulk-chemicals maker genuinely need different things, which keeps specialists alive. The likely middle ground is composable — a few big platform anchors plus best-of-breed pieces, stitched together through APIs and shared data.

The deepest trend is value moving to the decision layer. For thirty years software digitised the records (ERP), then the doing (TMS/WMS), then the seeing (visibility) — but each wave still left a human to make the actual call. Agentic AI goes after that last gap: if software can spot a problem, weigh the options against cost, service and carbon, and act, then the real prize becomes the orchestration layer and the proprietary data that trains it. That is why visibility networks, planning vendors and ERPs are all racing to own it — and why Gartner expects spend on agentic-AI supply-chain software to jump from under $2B to roughly $53B by 2030, with adoption climbing from about 5% to 60%.

Modes

Five ways to move the world's goods

Each transport mode has its own history, economics, data source, and geography. How they differ — and where each dominates.

⚑

The core trade-offChoosing a mode is the classic balance of cost vs. speed vs. reliability across a network of lanes. Ocean is the cheapest and slowest; air is the fastest and dearest; road is the flexible connective tissue between everything; rail is the efficient workhorse for bulk and long-haul; parcel is the e-commerce endgame at the doorstep. Most real shipments chain several modes together — the art is matching each leg to the goods.
